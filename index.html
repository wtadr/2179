01 The National Picture
A Crisis Growing Silently
Annual coastal drowning deaths 2019–2025

Coastal drowning deaths reached a record of 154 deaths in 2025 — a 22% rise in just two years. The 7-year average sits at 130 deaths per season.

Extended beach seasons, growing coastal tourism, and increasing numbers of overseas visitors unfamiliar with Australian surf conditions are all contributing factors.

Scale of Response
280 Incidents Stopped Per Rescue

For every person rescued in 2024, surf lifesavers stopped 280 other incidents before they became emergencies.

This number has grown every year since 2021, reflecting both the expanding scale of preventative patrols and the COVID dip in rescues rebounding more slowly than interventions.

Despite this extraordinary effort, deaths continue to rise — because most drownings occur entirely outside patrolled zones, beyond the reach of lifesavers.

Source: SLSA NCSR 2024–2025
Deadliest Activities
Swimming and Wading Account for More Than Half of Deaths

Swimming and wading were linked to 129 coastal drowning deaths across 2024–2025, representing more than half of all recorded fatalities.

Boating and watercraft activities were the next largest known contributor, while rock and land fishing, falls, and snorkelling/free diving each accounted for a similar share of deaths. Together, these figures show that drowning risk exists across a diverse range of coastal activities.

Swimming between the flags saves lives. Every single coastal drowning death in 2025 occurred outside patrolled areas, at unpatrolled beaches, or outside patrol hours. Not one death was recorded at a fully supervised flagged location during active patrol.
02 Who Is At Risk

Source: SLSA NCSR 2024
The Risk Profile

Males account for 86% of all coastal drowning deaths. This is consistent across all age groups and all years in the dataset.

The highest-risk age group is 40–49 year old males, followed closely by those aged 60–69. A secondary peak appears in young men aged 20–24, likely linked to risk-taking behaviour.

Older males (55+) accounted for 55% of all deaths in 2025 — a growing proportion as Australia's beach-going population ages.

Middle-aged men are the highest-risk group. Males aged 35–64 account for over half of all coastal drowning deaths — yet are the least likely to seek swimming lessons or heed beach safety warnings.
Overseas-Born Victims

Overseas-born residents and visitors are significantly over-represented in Australian drowning statistics.

In 2025, 33% of drowning victims were born overseas — nearly double their share of beach visitors. Many come from countries where open-water swimming is uncommon and rip current education is non-existent.

Rip currents cause the majority of surf rescues, yet three in four Australians cannot correctly identify a rip — for recent arrivals, the figure is far higher.

03 Where Danger Concentrates
Death Rate by State
Coastal drowning deaths per 100,000 population — bubble size and colour indicate rate severity, 2025

Tasmania recorded the highest per-capita coastal drowning rate in 2025, followed by South Australia.

NSW and QLD have the highest absolute counts, but large populations moderate their per-capita rates. Tasmania's small population amplifies the statistical impact of each death.

The NT's remote, unpatrolled coastline and extreme conditions create unique challenges for water safety intervention.

04 When Danger Strikes
Monthly Deaths: The Seasonal Clock
Coastal drowning deaths by month, 2025 (n=154) — radius = deaths
Source: SLSA NCSR 2025
Drowning Is Not Just a Summer Problem

Five months run hot. December and January lead as expected — peak summer, packed beaches, inexperienced swimmers. February and March follow close behind as the season winds down but the crowds remain.

April is the quiet alarm. Patrol seasons have ended in most states, yet deaths still spike above average — people swimming at beaches now empty of lifesavers.

From May through November, deaths fall below average. But they never reach zero.

Patrol seasons end. People don't stop swimming. The majority of off-season deaths involve adults swimming at locations that were patrolled just weeks earlier — now empty of lifesavers.
05 The Lifesaving Response

45,000 Volunteers Holding the Line

SLSA's patrolling membership has remained remarkably stable at 44,000–45,000 members across seven seasons — including through COVID-disrupted years.

This consistency reflects the deep cultural significance of surf lifesaving in Australia. The community of volunteers is estimated to contribute $9 billion in annual value to the nation through lives saved and injuries prevented.

Yet membership numbers have not kept pace with Australia's growing coastal population and visitor numbers — creating a widening gap between demand and available patrol coverage.

Many Treatments. Two Very Different Stories.

Queensland and NSW shoulder the overwhelming majority of marine sting and general first aid treatments — together accounting for the bulk of Australia's ~18,000 recorded cases in 2024.

This concentration reflects geography as much as population: QLD's warm, jellyfish-prone waters and dense tourist beaches drive treatment volumes that dwarf southern states by an order of magnitude.

Yet the clinical detail tells a quieter, sharper story. NT records disproportionately high cuts and abrasions relative to its coastal population — while CPR and fracture cases surface across every state, a reminder that serious incidents don't cluster where the crowds do.

Marine stings alone account for the majority of QLD's treatment load — a direct consequence of the state's 2,000-kilometre coastline and the seasonal presence of Irukandji and bluebottle jellyfish. For northern beachgoers, an encounter with marine life is statistically the most likely reason they'll need a lifesaver's attention.

The southern states skew differently. Victoria, SA, and Tasmania record modest totals — but their clinical mix leans heavier toward fractures and CPR. Colder water and rockier terrain mean fewer incidents overall, but more serious ones when they occur.

Behind every treatment is a patrol roster and a trained volunteer who arrived in time. The 18,000 figure is not just a count of the injured — it is a measure of how often the system worked.

Beach Hazard Ratings
Distribution of hazard ratings (1–10) for 61 major patrolled beaches, by state
Source: beachsafe.org.au
Not All Beaches Are Equal

Across 61 patrolled beaches, hazard ratings range from 2 to 8 — but the risk is anything but evenly spread. Victoria's Portsea Ocean Beach stands alone at a rating of 8, the highest in the dataset, while NT's Darwin beaches sit at the other end, rarely exceeding a 4.

NSW and QLD cluster toward the higher end, with several beaches rated 6 or 7 — Maroubra, Narrabeen, and Rainbow Beach among them. These are not just busy beaches; they are genuinely dangerous ones, where rips, swells, and marine hazards combine.

SA tells a quieter story. Its ten patrolled beaches sit almost entirely between 2 and 5 — calmer Gulf waters that reflect a fundamentally different coastal environment to the exposed ocean beaches of the east and south.

What the ratings don't capture is seasonality. NT's beaches are patrolled only from May to October — the dry season — while east coast patrols run through summer. A hazard rating of 4 means something very different when the surrounding conditions change entirely by month.

Each point represents one year of national lifesaving data. Point size reflects the volume of preventative actions that year.

Years with higher preventative action volumes tend to show proportionally lower rescue rates — suggesting that early intervention before emergencies saves more lives than rescue after the fact.

The 2024 point — largest and rightmost — represents a record year for both members and preventative actions, yet rescues also rose. More people visiting beaches means more demand, even as the workforce grows.
